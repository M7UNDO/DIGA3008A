Blog Post 10:Thinking About Justice and Ethics

Published on: 26 May 2025, by Mfundo Dhlamini

How can I approach justice and ethics in my artistic and programming practice?

When thinking about how I can approach justice and ethics in both my artistic and programming practice, I believe it's essential to be intentional about embedding values throughout the entire design process. Drawing inspiration from Values at Play: Design Tradeoffs in Socially Oriented Game Design (2005) by Flanagan et al., I've come to understand that justice and ethics are not just design goals, they're core considerations that shape how and why we create.

In both web development and game design I follow an iterative process constantly updating, testing, and reflecting on each iteration. In terms of game design, playtesting sessions help understand how players interact with my game, mechanics, and characters. I use these sessions to ask important questions, like is the experience inclusive for all kinds of players with different kinds of playstyles? Does the game effectively introduce players to the mechanics? Does the game encourage healthy or too much competition? Theses reflections allow me to stay aligned with the values like fairness, accessibility and diversity within my games.

In web development the iteration process feels different, but it is just as active. I experiment with different layouts and interaction styles and committing the versions I want users to engage with. I rely on these tests to ensure that my website supports values like trust, accessibly and clarity. The ethical challenges are different but just as important. One of the most practical ways I apply justice is by making sure all my pages on my website are accessible. This means applying semantic HTML wherever I can so screen readers can navigate through my content efficiently, I also make sure I use high contrast colour schemes for visibility and use relative units like rem for more responsive design in order to respect user preferences. These are not just technical best practices. They are intentional ethical choices that attempt to acknowledge the fact that not everyone use the web the same way, so my design choices can either include or exclude people .

I also try to make a point of avoiding dark patterns, that manipulate my users into doing things, like accidentally signing up for emails or making purchases they didn't mean to. In current and future design, I want to remain committed to staying away from these patterns in order to create websites that are honest and give user control of what they want to with their experience.

References

  • Flanagan, M., Howe, D.C. and Nissenbaum, H. (2005)Values at play: Tradeoffs in socially-oriented game design,' Human Factors in Computing Systems, 4(1),pp. 751-760. https://nyuscholars.nyu.edu/en/publications/values-at-play-tradeoffs-in-socially-oriented-game-design
