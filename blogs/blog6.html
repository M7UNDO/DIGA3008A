Blog Post 6:Digital Disenfranchisement: A Close Reading of Spyros Kasapis' Argument for Internet Access as a Fundamental Right (2021)

Published on: 28 April 2025, by Mfundo Dhlamini

Close Reading of Spyros Kasapis' “Internet Access Is a Fundamental Right. Especially Amidst a Pandemic

Kasapis opens the article by framing internet access within the broader tradition of human rights, drawing inspiration from Salvador Allende's claim that happiness and the basic needs that make happiness possible are human rights. Internet access is not positioned as a luxury but as an essential requirement for achieving dignity, equality, and full participation within a society. By making the connection between historical struggles for rights like education and healthcare, the article places internet access on the same level of urgency.

The core aim of the article is to make the argument that “free internet access should be indispensable to all members of a society (Kasapis, 2021). Having no access to the internet means being cut off from participating in society, as this restricts access to work, education, healthcare information and broader social participation. This is especially the case in light of the COVID-19 pandemic, which highlighted and intensified existing inequalities, as most aspects of our everyday lives shifted online, with companies and schools moving operations to platforms like Google Meet, Zoom and Teams (Case, 2020). Without digital devices or access to the internet, people were left behind. Students without internet access, fast internet speed, or reliable connection did not only lack access to education but also large information networks. In many underprivileged-to-participation areas, such as townships and rural areas, limited infrastructure became the primary barrier.

Poor connectivity, slow internet speeds, and unstable access made it extremely difficult to engage in online activities effectively. Measures of social distancing made participating in the digital economy something crucial, not optional, as even buying and selling moved online due to the cessation of human interaction (Lance Allen Liotta, 2023).

The article also highlights the emergence of digital inequality. By using Jeff Bezos' Amazon as an example for how capitalism boomed during this time as profits skyrocketed for the company due to an increase in online shopping, while half of the world's population still remained offline. This juxtaposition demonstrates that without the internet, billions are excluded from opportunities that are essential for survival today, such as education, healthcare and economic activities. Kasapis' claim is that our current economy denies billions access points to systemic global inequalities, where the internet is becoming as necessary as any basic need in our society (Kasapis, 2021).

In response to the urgent problem of digital inequality, the article raises important economic and political questions: Should all citizens be guaranteed internet access regardless of income? Should governments take responsibility for ensuring this right? And who should set the rules for the internet itself? Kasapis cautions that without public, private corporations could shape internet rules in ways that entrench existing inequalities, pointing us towards a more human rights-based framework that ensures fairness and democratic participation online (Kasapis, 2021).

Some avenues for future research that could be explored could be: Investigating how digital exclusion can amplify existing racial, gender, and economic inequalities Access to the internet is not just a technological constraint; it also interacts with various types of marginalisation and digital inequality. Examining legal frameworks for internet rights may also be worthwhile in order to establish how international human rights laws can evolve to explicitly include internet access and also identify which legal precedents (like the Indian Supreme Court ruling) can be leveraged across the globe.

In closing, I think the article makes a strong argument for reconsidering internet access as a fundamental human right, as opposed to a luxury or privilege. Kasapis effectively emphasises the problem of digital inequality, but he does not really suggest concrete models for how governments could provide free access. The article could've explored more solid models such as public-private partnerships and satellite initiatives to make the argument even stronger.
