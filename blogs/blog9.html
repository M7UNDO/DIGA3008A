Blog Post 9:Envisioning an Ethical Internet

Published on: 19 May 2025, by Mfundo Dhlamini

What an Ethical Internet looks like to me

In my view, an ethical internet must function as a truly inclusive public sphere, empowering diverse voices, prioritising universal access, fostering meaningful discourse, and resisting the forces of commercialisation and fragmentation. As Papacharissi highlights in “The Virtual Sphere: The Internet as a Public Sphere”, while the internet holds potential to revive public discourse, its current form often replicates and reinforces existing inequalities, creating a virtual space that is dominated by the economically and technologically privileged (Papacharissi 2020:21 ). This reflects Habermas's critique of the bourgeois public sphere, which was traditionally accessible only to property-owning males. Similar to today, disparities in infrastructure, affordability, and digital literacy persist, preventing marginalised communities from complete participation, despite the fact that global connectivity has reached 66% (ITU, 2023). An ethical internet must address these gaps through policy interventions(e.g broadband initiatives) and educational programmes that empower people to critically engage with digital content, since access alone is inadequate without the ability to navigate information responsibly (Schement & Curtis, 1997).

It's frustrating that decades after the internet's creation, we're still dealing with a digital divide where access depends on your income or where you live. Like Papacharissi points out, when only certain groups can participate, we end up with the same inequalities we see offline. In my understanding an ethical internet is one that bridges the bridge the digital divide, ensuring affordability and infrastructure for marginalised communities, as without this, online discourse remains “exclusive, elitist and far from ideal”, echoing Habermas's bourgeois public sphere (Papacharissi 2002: 14).

Additionally, commercialisation of the internet is also a concern. It's hard to ignore the fact that every social media platform seems to prioritise profit over their users. An ethical internet would make platforms and users accountable for the consequences of their digital actions. It would ensure that companies responsible for these platforms would be required to uphold a set of ethical standards, as opposed to profit driven algorithms. This would mean developing platform policies that actively combat disinformation, harassment, and exclusion, and ensure transparency in content moderation. Ultimately, an ethical internet is one that not only connects us, but also sustains justice, care, and inclusivity at its core.
