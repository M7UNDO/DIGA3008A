Blog 1: Reflecting on the Web and My Digital Journey

My chosen reading, The Library of Babel imagined a universe containing every book in the world, whether meaningful or not. Today, that universe fits in my pocket; my smartphone gives me access to infinite knowledge at the click of a button. The search for truth in Borges' The Library of Babel mirrors how I interact with the internet on a daily basis. However, there is one key difference. Instead of wandering through endless hexagonal rooms, I simply tap on a screen, and my phone connects me to nearly all recorded human knowledge. Whether I'm Googling for academic sources, checking and scrolling social media, or using AI for quick summaries, my phone functions as my own personal library. Today we are constantly bombarded with information, but in Borges' time, seeking knowledge was a slow and painstaking process (Borges, 2013).

The irony of having infinite information at my fingertips doesn't always translate to a deeper understanding. In the same way that Borges' librarians searched for a book that could explain everything, I do the same when I use Google to find answers. However, in a world where any information I am searching for is readily available, the true skill isn't just searching but knowing what to trust, what to question, and when to take a break from the never-ending flow of information.

Blog 2: Moulthrop, Hypertext, and Designing with Structure

Moulthrop's work remains a relevant critique of today's internet. Through his exploration of hypertext, he questioned how it would potentially change society and the media as we knew it. Moulthrop argued that while hypertext promised decentralisation and interaction, the reality would be shaped by corporate dominance and traditional models of broadcasting. As I consider how much the internet has developed, his insights seem almost prophetic; it is evident that concerns about corporate control and mass consumption have come true, as what was initially envisioned as an open, user-driven space has become largely controlled by major platforms like Google and Facebook (Meta), much like how television networks centralised media consumption before.

Through applying McLuhan's media framework to hypertext, Moulthrop argued that although hypertext was expected to replace books, it was meant to function more as an alternative to television and change the way that people consumed and produced content. Fast forward to today, where social media platforms, streaming platforms, and interactive digital spaces like online multiplayer games extend this same pattern. Offering users interaction within a structured and often corporate-controlled system.

Blog 5: Internet Access as a Fundamental Right: Bridging the Digital Divide in a Post-Pandemic World

This article really made me think about how intertwined internet access has become with everyday life and how unfair and unjust it is that so many people are still left out due to access. Kasapis makes a compelling argument for why internet access is not just a luxury or convenience; it should be a fundamental right that should be guaranteed to everyone, not just a tool that supports the rights of some people.

The COVID pandemic really highlighted this, as some of us were attending classes on Teams or Zoom, whereas others were completely cut off, especially in low-income communities like rural areas or townships. It's crazy to think about the fact that a significant portion of the world is still offline in 2025. According to the ITU, that number was 2.9 billion people as recently as 2021. This is more than a “digital divide”; it's a digital cliff. Another thing that stuck out for me in this article was the irony of tech billionaires raking in large profits while there are so many who still can't afford data to this day. Jeff Bezos grew his wealth astronomically during the period of COVID due to the increase in online shopping, while there were millions of people who could not access even the most basic digital resources. This is a reminder that the internet can be both a tool for equity or a force that can deepen divides and inequality , depending on the way that it is governed.

Blog 6: Digital Disenfranchisement: A Close Reading of Spyros Kasapis' Argument for Internet Access as a Fundamental Right (2021)

Kasapis opens the article by framing internet access within the broader tradition of human rights, drawing inspiration from Salvador Allende's claim that happiness and the basic needs that make happiness possible are human rights. Internet access is not positioned as a luxury but as an essential requirement for achieving dignity, equality, and full participation within a society. By making the connection between historical struggles for rights like education and healthcare, the article places internet access on the same level of urgency.

Blog 7: Beyond Access: Rethinking Digital Inequality Through a Structural Lens

My chosen reading is “Digital inequalities and why they matter” by Robinson et al. (2015). The article argues that digital inequality is not just about having access to technology or the internet, but it is also concerned with the quality, autonomy, skills, support, and purposes of digital engagement. Each of which reflects the existing broader structural inequalities in society. Before engaging with this reading, I had only understood digital inequality in binary terms (those who have internet access and those who do not) due to this being the primary discourse that surrounds it. Viewing digital inequality through this lens tends to overlook the more nuanced ways that technology intersects with social and economic power. Internet access alone is not enough, as having internet access does not automatically mean that someone is empowered or included in meaningful ways. This article points out that what matters is how people are using digital tools and what social conditions are shaping those uses.

Blog 9: Envisioning an Ethical Internet

In my view, an ethical internet must function as a truly inclusive public sphere, empowering diverse voices, prioritising universal access, fostering meaningful discourse, and resisting the forces of commercialisation and fragmentation. As Papacharissi highlights in “The Virtual Sphere: The Internet as a Public Sphere”, while the internet holds potential to revive public discourse, its current form often replicates and reinforces existing inequalities, creating a virtual space that is dominated by the economically and technologically privileged (Papacharissi 2020:21 ). This reflects Habermas's critique of the bourgeois public sphere, which was traditionally accessible only to property-owning males. Similar to today, disparities in infrastructure, affordability, and digital literacy persist, preventing marginalised communities from complete participation, despite the fact that global connectivity has reached 66% (ITU, 2023). An ethical internet must address these gaps through policy interventions(e.g broadband initiatives) and educational programmes that empower people to critically engage with digital content, since access alone is inadequate without the ability to navigate information responsibly (Schement & Curtis, 1997).

Blog 10: Thinking About Justice and Ethics

When thinking about how I can approach justice and ethics in both my artistic and programming practice, I believe it's essential to be intentional about embedding values throughout the entire design process. Drawing inspiration from Values at Play: Design Tradeoffs in Socially Oriented Game Design (2005) by Flanagan et al., I've come to understand that justice and ethics are not just design goals, they're core considerations that shape how and why we create.

In both web development and game design I follow an iterative process constantly updating, testing, and reflecting on each iteration. In terms of game design, playtesting sessions help understand how players interact with my game, mechanics, and characters. I use these sessions to ask important questions, like is the experience inclusive for all kinds of players with different kinds of playstyles? Does the game effectively introduce players to the mechanics? Does the game encourage healthy or too much competition? Theses reflections allow me to stay aligned with the values like fairness, accessibility and diversity within my games.
