Essay 2: The internet, geopolitics,
and digital coloniality

Published on: 12 June 2025, by Mfundo Dhlamini

In the digital age, internet access is no longer a luxury but a necessity for full participation within a society, enabling coordination, the exchange of information and even survival (Kasapis, 2021). The internet is not only a communication medium but also a geopolitical tool, and beneath the assurances of universal access and decentralised networks exists an infrastructure that is increasingly controlled by private entities, whose actions extend much beyond just business interests. This has never been more evident than in the recent geopolitical conflict between Russia and Ukraine, where US-based company SpaceX, headed by Elon Musk played a significant role in shaping the course of war and diplomacy through the deployment of Starlink, the largest satellite mega-constellation globally, delivering Internet service to remote regions.

While initially Starlink received praise and functioned as lifeline for Ukraine offering critical infrastructure in a crisis, the involvement of a private corporation in warfare have raised concerns about digital coloniality, where colonial power structures are being replicated by the technological dominance of Western entities.

This essay critically examines the intersections between internet governance, geopolitical power, and digital coloniality through the case study of SpaceX's Starlink satellite internet service in Ukraine between 2022 and 2024. By drawing on theories of digital geography as well as political economy this essay highlights how Starlink's intervention represents a paradigm shift in the way that digital infrastructure serving as both a tool of resistance and a means of neo-colonial domination. The analysis identifies key aspects of transformation which include: the privatisation of essential communication infrastructures in areas of conflict, the rise of corporate entities as geopolitical decision makers and the perpetuation of colonial power dynamic through technological dependence.

The Russian invasion of Ukraine in February of 2022 created demand for communication networks due to Russia disabling Ukrainian infrastructure through targeted assaults and cyberattacks. This left the Ukrainian government grappling with damaged infrastructure and disrupted communication. Following this, a decisive contribution that garnered a lot of publicity came from SpaceX, as Elon Musk announced on Twitter that Starlink would be made across Ukraine (Musk : 2022). In the following months Starlink was activated, and thousands of terminals across the country were deployed to support connectivity for the military, emergency services, and civilians (Abels 2024: 843). This intervention by the private sector filled a critical gap when the state and international responses received proved to be inadequate. Starlink provided encrypted, high-speed internet connectivity and quickly became a crucial tool for the Ukrainian military and government, making it indispensable as a communications infrastructure for the Ukrainian military (Jordan, 2023).

Bojor, Petrache and Cristescu (2024) note, by May 2022, over 150,000 Ukrainians were using Starlink on a daily basis. These terminals also assist in enabling a range of military applications, which include gathering intelligence, battle coordination and drone operations. This made Starlink an essential tool for operational efficiency and boosting morale between troops and their families, even under siege like in Mariupol's Azovstal plant (Bojor, Petrache and Cristescu, 2024).

What first seemed like an act of solidarity revealed its true intentions over time. In 2023 Musk revealed that he had refused a Ukrainian request to activate Starlink over Crimea, essentially preventing a drone strike on the Russian navy. This revelation proved that SpaceX engineers were making real time battlefield decisions about service availability, essential granting a private corporation veto power over military operations. This is an example of data colonialism where technological dependence creates a new form of extractive control. Musk's justification was that compliance would have made SpaceX explicitly complicit in the major war (Jordan, 2023). This unilateral decision shaped military strategy without the oversight of the state, demonstrating a private corporation exercising strategic autonomy and reconfiguring state business relations and positioning themselves as geopolitical actors.

Abels suggests that multinational corporations like SpaceX do not only function as commercial actors but also as geopolitical actors. This means their infrastructure control equates to political power. Their decision-making initially revolves around supplying critical infrastructure and later placing conditions on its use. This reveals a logic that is not entirely subordinate to state interests, but rather it represents a form of infrastructural power that intersects with global capital and strategic diplomacy (Abel, 2024).

Starlink's dual-use nature, used as both civilian infrastructure and a military asset, emphasised concerns of digital coloniality, as the dependency of a sovereign nation like Ukraine on a US-based corporation suggests an asymmetrical power relationship. In this case, digital coloniality emerges through technological dependence. This dynamic also undermines the principles of internet governance which are premised on state sovereignty. Greenstein, Forman and Goldfarb (2018) explain that the geography of internet infrastructure can exacerbate regional disparities. However, Starlink's orbital architecture bypasses terrestrial, allowing SpaceX to dictate its terms of use globally, essentially reshaping the political geography of cyberspace (Greenstein, Forman and Goldfarb, 2018).

Furthermore, this dynamic undermines the principles of internet governance premised on state sovereignty and multilateralism. As Greenstein et al. (2018) demonstrate, the geography of internet infrastructure can exacerbate regional disparities. Starlink's orbital architecture bypasses terrestrial jurisdiction, allowing SpaceX to dictate terms of access globally, effectively reshaping the political geography of cyberspace.

The intervention of Starlink in Ukraine challenges long-standing models of internet governance, which were rooted in cooperative, multi-stakeholder approaches. Although it is decentralised, its privately owned nature makes it difficult to regulate, while granting a great deal of control to its operator. Musk's frequent policy announcements on X (formerly Twitter) exemplify a form of “Twitter diplomacy”, where public and geopolitical decisions are made outside of formal channels and institutions” (Duncombe, 2017).

This shift raises profound ethical questions: Should a private company have the authority to decide the limits of military operations? What mechanisms exist to ensure accountability? These questions highlight the absence of existing internet governance frameworks to address the realities of privatised satellite constellations.

In response to these vulnerabilities, states and international bodies have begun to reassert control. The EU's IRIS2 and China's Guowang constellations represent attempts at being autonomous. Meanwhile the US has chosen to deepen their public-private partnerships with SpaceX, revealing the difficulty liberal democracies face when it comes to severing ties from dominant tech corporations (Abels, 2024). Recent developments highlight the fragility of this arrangement. On June 5, 2025, following his departure from DOGE and amid a feud with Donald Trump, Musk tweeted that SpaceX would immediately start decommissioning its Dragon spacecraft after Trump threatened to cancel his government contracts. Although he did reverse the decision hours later (Dinner, 2025). This instance highlights how tightly linked private corporations are with national concerns and this also reveals how volatile these ties can be, in situations like these where political disruption emerge. It raises the question about the risk of outsourcing critical infrastructure to private entities as their decisions could be influenced by their personal or corporate interests rather than national stability.

Ukraine remains in a vulnerable position, as it is still dependent on Starlink for its communications infrastructure, so it is still subject to its limitations. This further highlight the broader trend of private tech firm managing access to digital goods, essentially acting as gatekeepers to sovereignty.

Other countries have responded differently, for instance South Africa, which has notably declined licencing Starlink in the country. According to ICASA this decision was made based on SpaceX's refusal to comply with countries BBBEE requirement, that mandate 30% ownership to locally historically disadvantaged persons (Crouth, 2024). Moreover, the concerns about Starlink go beyond local licensing, and operating without physical presence in the country. It also speaks to the assertion of nation sovereignty over digital infrastructure and highlights the increasing awareness among countries from the Global South in understanding the risk associated with unregulated Western technological dominance.

In conclusion, this essay has critically examined how SpaceX's intervention of Starlink in Ukraine represents an intersection of internet governance, geopolitical power, and digital coloniality. By framing the analysis through theories of digital geography and political economy, this case reveals some of the ways in which digital infrastructure can serve as both a tool of resistance and hope but also a vehicle for neo-colonial influence. The role of Starlink, which went from supporting communication during a time of crisis to imposing unilateral decisions, perfectly illustrates the risks that are associated with privatised infrastructure, especially in conflict zones.
